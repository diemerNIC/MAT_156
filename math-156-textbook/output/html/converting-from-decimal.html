    Section 1.4 Converting from Decimal

    As we‚Äôve seen, converting from a different base back to decimal form can be done by expansion:
    \begin{align*} 316_8 \amp = 3\times8^2+1\times8^1+6\times8^0\\ \amp = 3\times64+1\times8+6\times1\\ \amp = 192+8+6\\ \amp=206 \end{align*}
    but how do we go back the other way? In order to do that, we first need to look as some modular arithmetic.

    Subsection 1.4.1 Modular Arithmetic: Finding Quotient and Remainder

    Suppose we wish to divide one integer by another. If the second integer doesn‚Äôt divide into the first evenly, the result is a real number which we can report either in fraction or decimal form. For example, if we divide 13 by 5, we get
    \begin{equation*} 13\div 5=\frac{13}{5}=2+\frac{3}{5}=2.6 \end{equation*}
    although the second last expression is usually written as \(2\frac{3}{5}\text{,}\) with the \(+\) symbol suppressed.
    If, for some reason, we wish to stay in the land of integers, we could report the result by saying that 13 divided by 5 equals 2, with 3 left over. In this example, the 2 is called the quotient and the 3 is called the remainder, and we can write this calculation in the following form:
    \begin{equation*} 13=\underbrace{2}_{\text{quotient}}\times 5 + \underbrace{3}_{\text{remainder}} \end{equation*}
    This sort of calculation is very helpful when doing unit conversions.‚Äâ1‚Äâ For example, if we know that a certain length of time is 54 hours long and we would prefer to give it in terms of days and hours, then
    \begin{align*} 54 \text{ hours} \amp =\underbrace{\text{days}}_{\text{quotient}}\times 24+\underbrace{\text{hours}}_{\text{remainder}}\\ \amp = 2\times 24+6 \end{align*}
    so 54 hours \(=\) 2 days plus 6 hours.
    But how can we find quotients and remainders with a standard calculator? If we take the number 54 and divide it by 24, our calculator will tell us 2.25. There are two ways to go:
    1. Take the integer part of 2.25, which is 2. Then perform the following calculation:
      \begin{align*} \text{remainder} \amp = 54-\underbrace{2}_{\text{integer part}}\times 24\\ \amp = 54-48\\ \amp = 6 \end{align*}
    2. Alternatively, you can take the decimal part of 2.25, which is 0.25, and multiply it by the divisor, 24:
      \begin{align*} \text{remainder} \amp = \underbrace{0.25}_{\text{decimal part}}\times 24\\ \amp = 6 \end{align*}

    Example 1.4.1.

    Find the quotient and remainder for the following:
    1. \(\displaystyle \ 25\div 4\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \ 86\div 3\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \ 101\div 12\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \ 91\div 8\)
    Solution.
    1. \begin{align*} \amp 25\div 4=6.25\\ \amp \text{quotient }=\text{integer part of }6.25=6\\ \amp \text{remainder }=25-\underbrace{6}_{\text{integer part}} \times 4=1\\ \amp \text{or remainder }=\underbrace{0.25}_{\text{decimal part}}\times 4=1 \end{align*}
    2. \(\displaystyle \text{ quotient }=28,\text{ remainder }=2\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \text{ quotient }=8,\text{ remainder }=5\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \text{ quotient }=11,\text{ remainder }=3\)

    Subsection 1.4.2 Using Quotient and Remainder to Convert from Decimal Form

    Now that we know how to find quotients and remainders, let‚Äôs look at some examples that convert from a decimal number into octal. Consider the number \(316_8\text{.}\) We earlier found that \(316_8=206_{10}\text{.}\) Let‚Äôs now go back the other way, using repeated division and remainders.

    Example 1.4.2.

    Convert the decimal number 206 to octal.
    The procedure is to divide 206 by the base, which in this case is 8, and write down the quotient and the remainder. Then divide the quotient by the base and write down the new quotient and remainder. As you can see below, we first divide 206 by 8 to get a quotient of 25 with remainder 6. If we then divide 25 by 8, we get a quotient of 3 with remainder 1. we continue doing this until we get a quotient of zero, as in the table below.
    quotient remainder
    \(206\div 8=\) 25 6
    \(25\div 8=\) 3 1
    \(3\div 8 =\) 0 3
    Taken in reverse order, the remainders comprise the digits of the equivalent octal number \(316_8\text{.}\)
    Let‚Äôs do some more examples using this same procedure.

    Example 1.4.3.

    Convert the decimal number 41 to binary.
    Solution.
    quotient remainder
    \(41\div 2=\) 20 1
    \(20\div 2=\) 10 0
    \(10\div 2 =\) 5 0
    \(5\div 2=\) 2 1
    \(2\div 2=\) 1 0
    \(1\div 2=\) 0 1
    Reading the remainders from bottom to top gives \(41_{10}=101001_2\)

    Example 1.4.4.

    Convert the decimal number 24362 to hexadecimal.
    Solution.
    quotient remainder (base 10) remainder (base 16)
    \(24362\div 16=\) 1522 10 A
    \(1522\div 16=\) 95 2 2
    \(95\div 16=\) 5 15 F
    \(5\div 16=\) 0 5 5
    So \(24362_{10}=5F2A_{16}\)

    Subsection 1.4.3 Converting Non-integer Numbers from Decimal Form

    We have just seen that to convert an integer number from decimal form, we divide by the base repeatedly. To convert the fractional part of a non-integer decimal number to another base, we instead will multiply the base repeatedly.

    Example 1.4.5.

    Convert the decimal number 0.59375 to octal.
    The procedure is to multiply the decimal number by the base, and split the result into the integer part and the fractional (decimal) part. Then take the fractional part and write it on the next line. Mulitply it by the base (8 in this example) and split as before. Stop when the fractional part goes to zero. Finally, write down the integer parts from top to bottom after the radix point and affix the subscript to indicate the base.
    integer fractional
    \(0.59375\times 8=\) \(4\) \(+0.75\)
    \(0.75\times 8=\) \(6\) \(+0\)
    Reading the integer parts from top to bottom gives \(0.59375_{10}=0.46_8\text{.}\)

    Example 1.4.6.

    Convert the decimal number 0.625 to binary.
    Solution.
    integer fractional
    \(0.625\times 2=\) \(1\) \(+0.25\)
    \(0.25\times 2=\) \(0\) \(+0.5\)
    \(0.5\times 2=\) \(1\) \(+0\)
    We have \(0.625_{10}=0.101_2\text{.}\)

    Example 1.4.7.

    Convert the decimal number 0.6328125 to hexadecimal.
    Solution.
    integer fractional
    \(0.6328125\times 16=\) \(10 (A)\) \(+0.125\)
    \(0.125\times 16=\) \(2\) \(+0\)
    Reading the integer parts from top to bottom gives \(0.6328125_{10}=0.A2_{16}\)
    Let‚Äôs do an example with a twist.

    Example 1.4.8.

    Convert the decimal number 0.3 to octal.
    Solution.
    integer fractional
    \(0.3\times 8=\) \(2\) \(+0.4\)
    \(0.4\times 8=\) \(3\) \(+0.2\)
    \(0.2\times 8=\) \(1\) \(+0.6\)
    \(0.6\times 8=\) \(4\) \(+0.8\)
    \(0.8\times 8=\) \(6\) \(+0.4\)
    You‚Äôll notice that we have a bit of a problem: if we put the fractional/decimal part 0.4 on the next line, then we‚Äôll have a repeat of the second line, and then the next three lines will also repeat, and so on. What this means is that \(0.3_{10}\) is a repeating decimal in octal: \(0.3_{10}=0.2\overline{3146}_8\text{.}\)
    Finally, what if we are converting a non-integer number that has both an integer part and a fractional part? The answer is to do the two parts separately and then put them together.

    Example 1.4.9.

    Convert the number 17.375 to binary.
    Solution.
    First, we‚Äôll do the integer part and convert \(17_{10}\) to binary.
    quotient remainder
    \(17\div 2=\) \(8\) \(1\)
    \(8\div 2=\) \(4\) \(0\)
    \(4\div 2=\) \(2\) \(0\)
    \(2\div 2 = \) \(1\) \(0\)
    \(1\div 2=\) \(0\) \(1\)
    So \(17_{10}=10001_2\)
    Now, convert \(0.375_{10}\) to binary.
    integer fractional
    \(0.375\times 2=\) \(0\) \(+0.75\)
    \(0.75\times 2=\) \(1\) \(+0.5\)
    \(0.5\times 2=\) \(1\) \(+0.0\)
    So \(0.375_{10}=0.011_2\)
    Putting it all together, we get that \(17.375_{10}=10001.011_2\)
