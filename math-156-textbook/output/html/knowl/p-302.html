However, let us now consider sequences that start with zero:
\begin{equation*} a_0, a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots, a_n \end{equation*}
Numbering the terms, we find that
\begin{equation*} \overbrace {{a_0}}^{{\rm{first}}},\overbrace {{a_1}}^{{\rm{second}}},\overbrace {{a_2}}^{{\rm{third}}},\overbrace {{a_3}}^{{\rm{fourth}}},\overbrace {{a_4}}^{{\rm{fifth}}}, \ldots ,\overbrace {{a_n}}^{{\rm{final}}} \end{equation*}
and \(a_5\) is no longer the fifth term. In fact, \(a_5\) is the sixth term, which is why it is common in programming to separate the “count” of a term (first, second, third, etc.) from the index value (0 for \(a_0\text{,}\) etc.).
